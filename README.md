# Project 5: Pay Equity Analysis Dashboard

## Overview

This project replicates a professional pay equity audit using a synthetic dataset of 1,000 employees. It decomposes the gender pay gap into its structural and direct components using OLS regression, compa-ratio analysis, and pay band compliance reporting — following the methodologies published by Mercer and Willis Towers Watson.

The analysis is structured to mirror what HR practitioners and compensation professionals actually do in a formal pay equity review, making the work directly relevant to People Analytics and HRIS roles.

---

## Research Foundation

### Mercer Global Pay Equity Report
Mercer's research documents an unadjusted global gender pay gap of approximately 18%. When controlling for job grade, function, and location, the gap narrows to 2 to 3 percent. This finding exposes structural placement inequity — not direct pay discrimination — as the dominant driver of total compensation differences. The adjusted gap is the number that matters most in a formal audit because it isolates factors that compensation policy can actually address.

### Willis Towers Watson Pay Equity Audit Framework
WTW's three-stage audit methodology moves from job architecture through market pricing to regression analysis. The primary equity metric in this framework is the compa-ratio, defined as actual salary divided by the pay range midpoint. WTW defines a target equity band of 0.85 to 1.15. Any employee below 0.90 is flagged for corrective review. WTW's research also found that approximately 66 percent of total pay inequity is explained by grade distribution differences rather than direct pay decisions within the same role. This finding shapes the structure of this entire project.

## Dataset Design

The synthetic dataset is generated by `pay_equity_generator.py` and produces 1,000 employee records calibrated to the benchmarks above.

### Columns

| Column | Description |
|---|---|
| employee_id | Unique identifier (EMP10010 to EMP11010) |
| gender | Male, Female, or Non-binary |
| ethnicity | Six categories including White, Black or African American, Hispanic or Latino, and Asian |
| age | Employee age (22 to 62) |
| location | Toronto, Vancouver, Calgary, Ottawa, Montreal, or Remote |
| is_remote | Binary flag (1 = remote) |
| department | Eight departments including Engineering, Finance, HR, and Sales |
| job_title | Role within department |
| job_grade | G1 (Entry) through G6 (Director+) |
| job_level | Text description of the grade |
| hire_date | Date of joining (between 2010 and 2024) |
| years_tenure | Years at the company as of June 2024 |
| years_experience | Total career experience including prior roles |
| performance_rating | 1 to 5 scale (distribution: 3% at 1, 50% at 3, 7% at 5) |
| promoted_last_2yr | Binary flag (1 = promoted in last two years) |
| base_salary | Annual base salary in CAD |
| range_min | Minimum of the grade pay band |
| range_mid | Midpoint of the grade pay band |
| range_max | Maximum of the grade pay band |
| compa_ratio | base_salary divided by range_mid (WTW primary metric) |
| range_penetration | Position within band (0 = at min, 0.5 = at mid, 1 = at max) |
| in_band_flag | 1 if salary is within the grade band, 0 if outside |
| bonus_pct | Bonus as a percentage of base salary |
| bonus_amount | Bonus in CAD |
| total_comp | Base salary plus bonus |
| last_increase_pct | Most recent merit increase as a percentage |

### Pay Band Structure (CAD)

| Grade | Level | Min | Midpoint | Max |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| G1 | Entry | 45,000 | 55,000 | 65,000 |
| G2 | Junior | 60,000 | 72,000 | 85,000 |
| G3 | Mid-Level | 80,000 | 95,000 | 110,000 |
| G4 | Senior | 100,000 | 120,000 | 140,000 |
| G5 | Lead / Manager | 130,000 | 152,000 | 175,000 |
| G6 | Director+ | 160,000 | 190,000 | 225,000 |

### Pay Gap Mechanics

The dataset encodes two layers of pay inequity:

**Layer 1 — Structural (grade clustering).** Female and underrepresented minority employees are assigned to lower grades at higher rates. This replicates WTW's finding that grade placement differences explain approximately 66 percent of total pay inequality. The effect is calibrated through separate grade probability distributions by demographic group.

**Layer 2 — Direct (within-grade).** Within the same grade, female and non-binary employees receive a salary multiplier of 0.965 to 0.990 relative to male peers. Underrepresented minority employees receive a multiplier of 0.972 to 0.995. These ranges replicate Mercer's 2 to 3 percent adjusted gender pay gap.

The combination of both layers produces a realistic unadjusted pay gap of approximately 14 to 17 percent in generated data.

## How to Run

### Generate the dataset

```bash
pip install pandas numpy openpyxl faker
python pay_equity_generator.py
```

This creates `pay_equity_data.csv` and `pay_equity_data.xlsx` in the same directory.

### Run the notebook

```bash
pip install jupyter matplotlib seaborn statsmodels
jupyter notebook pay_equity_notebook.ipynb
```

### Run the SQL queries

Load `pay_equity_data.csv` into a PostgreSQL database as the `pay_equity_data` table, then execute the queries in `pay_equity_analysis.sql`. All queries are compatible with PostgreSQL 13 and above.

### Connect to Power BI

1. Open Power BI Desktop and import `pay_equity_data.xlsx` or `pay_equity_data.csv`.
2. Open the Modeling tab and create a New Measure.
3. Paste each measure from `pay_equity_dax_measures.txt` into the formula bar.
4. Build the 5 report pages using the measures as described below.

## Power BI Dashboard Pages

### Page 1: Executive Summary
KPI cards for total headcount, unadjusted gender pay gap, average compa-ratio by gender, in-band compliance rate, and the count of employees below the 0.90 compa-ratio risk threshold. A donut chart shows gender and ethnicity split. This page is the starting point for leadership presentations.

### Page 2: Compa-Ratio Analysis
KDE chart of compa-ratio distribution by gender with WTW band boundary markers at 0.85 and 1.15. Heatmap of average compa-ratio by job grade and gender. Bar chart ranking departments by average compa-ratio. Slicer for gender and grade.

### Page 3: Pay Band Compliance
Table showing in-band percentage by department and grade. Scatter chart of individual employees by range penetration and grade, colored by gender. Allows drilling into out-of-band employees for corrective review.

### Page 4: Adjusted vs. Unadjusted Gap
Waterfall or bar chart decomposing the total pay gap into structural (grade clustering) and direct (within-grade) components. Line chart of female grade share by grade, showing the concentration pattern. A call-out card displays the adjusted gap percentage versus the Mercer benchmark.

### Page 5: Employee Drill-Through
Configured as a drill-through page from any other page. Shows individual employee salary position, compa-ratio, peer average for the same grade and department, and pay gap to peer. Used during corrective review sessions.

## Key Findings (from Notebook)

The following findings are produced by running `pay_equity_notebook.ipynb` on the generated dataset. Exact values vary slightly between runs due to random seed effects.

The unadjusted gender pay gap is approximately 15 percent, consistent with Mercer's global benchmark of 18 percent. After controlling for job grade in the regression model, the gap narrows to approximately 5 to 7 percent, confirming that grade clustering is the primary driver. The full audit model — controlling for grade, department, performance rating, and tenure — reduces the residual gap to approximately 2 to 3 percent, matching Mercer's adjusted gap benchmark.

The compa-ratio analysis shows a gap of approximately 0.03 to 0.05 between male and female employees, with women averaging below the midpoint in G1 through G3. Approximately 15 to 20 percent of female employees fall below the 0.90 compa-ratio threshold, compared to approximately 8 to 10 percent of male employees.

The structural component of the gap accounts for roughly 60 to 70 percent of the total, consistent with WTW's 66 percent finding.
